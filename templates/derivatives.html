    Derivatives

    The derivative is the instantaneous rate of change at which output of a function changes as its input value changes. The rate of change of a function \(f(x)\), often denoted as \(\dfrac{d}{x}\left(f(x)\right)\), \(\dfrac{df}{dx}\), \({D}{f}\left(x\right)\), or \({f'}\left(x\right) = 0\). Derivative is used to optimize a function also it find the intervals where the function is increasing or decreasing.
    Given below are the basic rules of derivatives:

    Constant Rule

    According to the derivatives constant rule, the derivatives of a constant is always be 0. If \(y = c\), where \(c\) is a constant, then \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{dc}{dx} = 0\).
    Example: \(y = 20\)
    \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{d}{dx}\left(20\right) = 0\).

    Constant Multiplication Rule

    It states that if you have a function that is a constant multiplied by another function, the derivative of this expression is the constant multiplied by the derivative of the other function. If \(y = cx\), where \(c\) is a constant, then \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\left(cx\right) = c \cdot \dfrac{d}{dx}\left(x\right)\).
    Example: \(y = 7x\)
    \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{d}{dx}\left(7\right)\)

    \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 7 \cdot \dfrac{d}{dx}\left(x\right)\)         (i.e    \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\left(x\right) = 1\))

    \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 7\)

    Power Rule

    According to power rule, if we have \(y = x^n\), then \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = n \cdot x^{(n-1)}\).
    Example: \(y = x^3\)
    \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{d}{dx}\left(x^3\right)\)

    \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 3 \cdot x^{3-1}\)

    \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 3 \cdot x^{2}\)

    Example: \(y = u^2(x)\)

    \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 2u(x) \cdot \dfrac{du}{dx}\)

    Sum/Difference Rule

    According to sum and difference rule, the derivative process distributed over addition as subtraction as: \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} [a \pm b] \) = \(\dfrac{da}{dx} \pm \dfrac{db}{dx}\).
    Example: \(y = 2x \pm 7\)

    \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{d}{dx}\left(2x \pm 7\right)\)

    \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{d}{dx}\left(2x\right) \pm \dfrac{d}{dx}\left(7\right) \)

    \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 2 \pm 0 \)

    \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 2 \)

    Product Rule

    The rule for finding the derivative of a product of two functions states that, “the derivative of the second function multiplied by the first function added to the derivative of the first function multiplied by the second function.” Such as \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\left(a \times b \right) = a \cdot \dfrac{db}{dx} + b \cdot \dfrac{da}{dx} \)    ("." is alternatively used for multiplication).
    Example: \(y = 3x^3 \times 5x\)

    \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{d}{dx}(3x^3 \times 5x)\)

    \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 3x^3 \cdot \dfrac{d}{dx}(5x) + 5x \cdot 3\dfrac{d}{dx}(x^3)\)

    \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 3x^3 \cdot 5 + 5x \cdot 3 \times 3 x^2\)

    \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 3x^3 \cdot 5 + 9 x^2 \cdot 5x \)

    Trigonometric Functions

    These derivative formulas are essential for finding the rates of change of trigonometric functions and their inverses in calculus and physics problems.

    \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}(sin(x)) = cos(x)\)

    \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}(cos(x)) = -sin(x)\)

    \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}(tan(x)) = sec^2(x)\)

    \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}(sin^{-1}(x)) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}}\)

    \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}(cos^{-1}(x)) = -\frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}}\)

    \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}(tan^{-1}(x)) = \frac{1}{{1 + x^2}}\)
